The Cascade

Your work flows through a five-role cascade. Quality gate before you see anything.

Most AI tools give you one model and one perspective. Stew runs a cascade — Intake sorts your request, Master plans the work, Senior does the heavy lifting, Quality checks it, Junior files it. The Quality gate fails work back to Senior if it's not right. You see only what passes.

The check step is the moat. Quality always sees the work before you do. If something is wrong, Quality sends it back to Senior — not to you. You only see deliverables that have passed an independent check.

Same cascade. Any scale.

There's no headcount limit. The Master plans as many Seniors as the work needs. Each Senior plans as many Juniors as their sub-area needs. Quality checks all of it. Your Stew scales to the problem, not the other way around.

How is this different from one AI doing everything?

One AI handling everything is one perspective, one judgment call, one failure point. Stew's cascade splits the work the way a competent organization does — someone plans, someone executes, someone checks, someone files. The check step catches mistakes before they reach you.

How do I know it's not overkill for small things?

The cascade scales down as easily as it scales up. A simple question routes through one of each role and finishes in seconds. A massive project routes through dozens of Seniors and hundreds of Juniors and finishes in hours instead of months. You don't choose the scale — the work does.

How does Stew pick which AI does which role?

Among the AIs you've connected to your Stew, the best one for each job class gets the work. A premium reasoning model might handle the Master role; a fast cheap model might handle Intake or Junior. The routing is automatic and tuned for quality first, cost second.
